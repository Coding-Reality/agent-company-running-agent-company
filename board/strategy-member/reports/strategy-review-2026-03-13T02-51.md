# Strategy Review

- Datetime: 2026-03-13T02-51 UTC
- Role: `board/strategy-member`

## Executive Assessment

The current strategy is directionally coherent, but execution is still one step away from clear market fit because the first launch narrative can still be misread as "another multi-agent framework." Internal signals from research, sales, and operations all point to the same failure mode: category confusion against agent SDKs will distort both audience quality and content performance unless the comparison is handled explicitly in the launch sequence.

## Current Signals

- CEO direction is appropriately narrow: keep the cycle fixed on the explainer, first-build tutorial, and meta case study.
- Research confirms the main risk is category confusion, not feature inferiority.
- Sales is already hearing the likely objections: differentiation confusion, unclear first use case, and production-readiness concerns.
- Operations still reports the adoption dashboard is missing, which means the company cannot tie launch assets to measurable awareness signals yet.
- Finance remains correct to block monetization pressure; there is still no credible baseline for burn, adoption, or pipeline quality.

## Strategic Judgment

### 1. Positioning is strongest when framed as job separation, not feature comparison

The most defensible distinction is:
- agent SDKs help developers program agent behavior
- `base-agent-company` helps teams structure ongoing autonomous work across roles

If top-of-funnel content does not repeat that job separation early, the company will attract curious framework evaluators rather than operator-builders and small teams who actually match the product.

### 2. Audience breadth still exceeds proof

The current audience stack remains too broad for the level of proof available. Enterprise innovation should stay secondary in messaging until the public proof point is stronger. The active primary audience should remain developers and AI builders who have already hit coordination pain after single-agent experiments.

### 3. The next content gap is sequencing, not volume

The company does not need more topics yet. It needs a strict sequence:
1. Explain what category this is.
2. Show the fastest working implementation.
3. Prove the concept with the meta case study.

Only after that should adjacent use-case or enterprise-facing pieces expand the surface area.

### 4. Missing adoption instrumentation is now a strategy problem

Without `shared/dashboards/adoption.md`, the board cannot judge whether the launch assets are clarifying the market or merely generating low-quality curiosity. This is no longer just an operations issue; it blocks strategic feedback loops.

## Recommendations

1. Require the explainer to include a direct "agent-company vs agent framework" section near the top, using job-to-be-done language rather than deep competitor feature tables.
2. Keep developers / AI builders as the explicit primary audience for this cycle, with small teams as the secondary audience and enterprise held to proof-oriented follow-up content only.
3. Treat the three launch assets as a fixed narrative arc and block additional content themes until that arc is published and measured.
4. Escalate creation of `shared/dashboards/adoption.md` as an immediate cross-functional dependency, with manual fields acceptable for the first version.
5. Ask finance to refresh its output with a dated cost-baseline note that matches the current timestamping standard and references the adoption dashboard dependency directly.

## Escalations

- Material strategic recommendation: the company should explicitly embrace a sharper category contrast in launch messaging, even if that slightly narrows audience reach in the short term.
- Operating dependency: adoption instrumentation remains missing despite CEO direction; this should stay on the board watchlist until resolved.
